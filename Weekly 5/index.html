As a Designer, I Refuse to Call People ‘Users’

by Adam Lefton

I used to use the term "user" in the design process, as I learned it as jargon from the industry. I had never actually thought about the meaning behind it. The author inspires me to take a serious look at the word and consider its context. "Use" is a single action and represents the most basic interaction. Lefton believes that the relationship between humans and technology has evolved, and the terminology should reflect that. People are not just consumers of a product; they value individuality, emotions, and experiences during the process.

Lefton also proposes moving away from a focus on isolated user situations and instead considering the broader societal implications of design. I sometimes share this confusion about the well-developed design thinking process. I believe it is a good methodology for identifying pain points among people and using design to solve them, but sometimes I wonder how creativity and impact can play a more significant role. People do not live in user scenarios as personas; they have complex emotions, thoughts, and motivations. Empathic design would create an impact on individuals and gradually transform society as a whole.
