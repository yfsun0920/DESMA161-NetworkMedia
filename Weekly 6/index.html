Informatic Opacity

by Zach Blas

The author discussed the implications of surveillance and visibility. Many ideas resonate with the discourse surrounding privacy and identity under the age of artificial intelligence. Data are extracted and analyzed in a more automatic process to form models of behavior and identity. These models are shaped by biases, and continue to reinforce the current norms as well as marginalize outside the expected patterns. Standing at the intersection of art and technology, I see artists experiment to challenge the dominance and surveillance.

Tech companies own our data and provide content within the usersâ€™ preference based on recommendation algorithms. Today these recommendations are surprisingly accurate. Personally I do not like these kinds of recommendation. I remembered few years before I often try to search topics that I have no interest or knowledge about in order to balance the recommendation algorithms. I want to see diverse content from the digital space. I do not know whether creating a more accurate model with data narrows down human view. It seems that we are already in the models and cannot think without them.

Recently I have also seen an interesting project of adding a filter to images to prevent it from studies by image generation models. Similar to the artworks mentioned in the article, these engineers are working on a new mask in the virtual space trying to maintain the integrity of images and visuals.
