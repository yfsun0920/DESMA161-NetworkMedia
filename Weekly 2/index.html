How to Internet

by Jenny Odell

The "American Highway" metaphor has been used to describe early internet infrastructure. This metaphor evolved into the "Information Superhighway" to emphasize the transformative potential of the internet in connecting people, information, and resources worldwide, much like a physical highway connects cities and towns.

The metaphor still applies today, as we interpret the internet experience in the context of big data and algorithms. If the internet is a superhighway, it seems like there are fewer exits, and users are expected to keep traveling to the same destinations repeatedly, based on their past behavior. People today don't need the metaphor to understand the concept of the internet because they're accustomed to this experience and rely on it as part of daily life. However, it required a lot of effort for people in the past to understand something so new. Early software aimed to help users build their imagination by equating the internet experience with the physical world. The computer desktop was reimagined as a house with rooms and physical objects.

"How to Everything" provides instructions for emerging technologies and experiences. "How to Internet" transforms into "How to Everything" by learning on the internet. Businesses discovered opportunities around this and disrupted the freedom and randomness of the early internet. It's difficult to return to that time, and freedom and randomness don't always equate to positive outcomes. To understand "how to internet" probably requires exploring a variety of connectivities.

A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace

by John Perry Barlow

The document served as a firm belief of the potential of cyberspace as a realm free from traditional governmental control and regulation. Barlow's vision for cyberspace was one where individuals could engage in free expression, without limitations and prejudices of the physical world. He argued that the governments of the world had no moral authority to impose their rules on this new frontier, as cyberspace was created by a social contract of its users and existed outside the borders of nation-states.

As the internet has become increasingly regulated and controlled, the declaration seemed to be a reminder of the ideals and aspirations that characterized the early days of the internet.
